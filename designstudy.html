1. Learning

As traditional energy sources such as coal and natural gas are running out, it is important to research into and utilize renewable energy sources. Renewable sources can be replenished, while non-renewables are finite. 85% of the US's energy usage is non-renewable (U.S. Energy Information Administration). It is believed that oil and gas will run out by the end of the century (BP Statistical Review of World Energy, 2020).

In our tool, we would like to focus on how states use renewable energy, trends of renewable energy usage by state and year, and use it to help increase adoption of renewable sources. By looking at historical data, we can identify states who are doing well in renewable energy adoption, and use them as models when creating next generation energy policies that will increase renewable energy adoption countrywide.

2. Winnowing

The Data

  • Energy data from the US Government
    • Historical data from 1960 to 2019, reported as integers and floats
    • Cases without data are reported as 0.
  • Variables include consumption, expenditures, production and prices of different fuel sources over the years in different states
    • Over 3000 rows
    • Consolidate renewable and non-renewable fuel sources
  • Renewable vs Non-Renewable
    • Renewable: geothermal, solar, hydropower, wind, wood
    • Non-renewable: coal, distillate fuel oil, kerosene, petroleum, natural gas, and other petroleum products
    • Sourced from energy.gov

Task One

On a state-by-state basis, what energy sources are used the most?
  • Goal: Discover the proportion of energy sources for each state, to determine if certain ones that are not renewable can be reduced
  • Means: Plot the amount of each energy type used for each state.
  • Characteristics: Learn what energy sources are dominant in each state
  • Target data: The given energy dataset, the State and Consumption fields
  • Workflow: After data for most recent year becomes available.
  • Roles: The team hired by the Department of Energy.

Task Two

Which states in the United States are using more renewable forms of energy?
  • Goal: Discover which states are doing the most to improve their renewability
  • Means: Categorize the energy sources by renewability. Compare different states
  • Characteristics: Learn which states are utilizing the most renewable energy in proportion to non-renewable energy
  • Target data: The given energy dataset, the State and Consumption fields. External data used to quantify renewability
  • Workflow: After data for most recent year becomes available.
  • Roles: The team hired by the Department of Energy.

4. Design

Design #1

  • Focus on a comparison of different states' consumption in a particular year
  • Provide a comparison between renewable and non-renewable energy sources

Design #2

  • Focus on consumption for a state and fuel source over the years
  • Split the types of energy specifically, not just renewable and nonrenewable

Design #3

  • Focus on comparing consumption of different energy types by a specified state
  • Provide more detailing on which fuel sources a state consumes most
  • Provide clear distinction between renewable and non-renewable sources

8. Reflect Part 1

Reflection

  • Most states have numbers for non-renewable energy usage that dwarfs renewable, but most states are making progress in utilizing non-renewable energy (although not enough)
  • In some cases, it's a factor of hundreds to one
  • Maine seems to be far and away the best when it comes to proportional usage
  • States with the highest non renewable energy usage per capita are states with low populations and large fuel-based industries, such as Alaska
  • Even after accounting for population, Texas nonrenewable usage is still high
  • To ensure long-term fuel usability, states should try to bring their renewable and nonrenewable usage closer to parity.

Policy Recommendations

1. When choosing states as a model: we can look at Maine.
  • In the most recent years, Maine used renewable energy ~3 times less than non-renewable energy. It used it be ~7 times less in the 1970s
  • Maine uses wood as their 2nd most used fuel source
  • Recommend studying further into how Maine is able to utilize renewable energy
  • Is this possible for other states? Maine has a low population (1.4 million) and 90% of Maine is forested (Maine TREE Organization)
2. Texas used 224.95 times more non-renewable energy than renewable in 2019.
  • 6th highest non-renewable energy usage value in the country per capita
  • Highest non-renewable usage in 2019: Petroleum (7 quadrillion BTU)
  • Highest renewable usage in 2019: Wood (64 trillion BTU)
3. To improve Texas's fuel usage, money should be divested from the petroleum industry and towards industries like wood and solar that offer long-term renewability.
  • Difficult, as petroleum is a large part of Texas's economy
4. We need to account for state population and sources available in the state because renewable sources are dependent on climate, geography and vegetation.

9. Reflect Part 2

Reflecting on our Tool

  • It is important to select relevant variables from the dataset and particularly compare energy usage through time - can see progress is being made even though it is slow
  • Sorting really helps with direct comparisons between qualitative categories and when there are a lot of things to compare
  • Need to showcase a lot of data points and make sure it is user friendly and explorable to people with not much background in energy (like policy makers)
  • Difficult to make visualizations that visually convey the information efficiently.
    • Had some difficulty on the multiple fuel sources plot, conveying the energy sources at the bottom of the plot.
    • Solution: Remove data points below a given threshold. But what's a good trade-off between omitting information and readability?
